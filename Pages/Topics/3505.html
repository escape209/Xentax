Does anyone know of any instances of games companies specifically attacking (either with take down notices/legal threats or polite requests) modders and people reverse engineering their games?

The ones I am aware of:
Blizzard attacking bnetd
Blizzard attacking people modding WoW (because of the issue that any mods for MMOs can be considered cheating)
EA Sports attacking at least one modder of one of their Formula 1 titles because they were putting newer season drivers/teams/tracks/data into an older title (so depriving EA of revenue from the newer titles)
EA attacking people modding the scripting for one of the Sims titles because people were making all sorts of new objects and thus making the many expansion packs a lot less attracting to players or something)
Telltale going after someone who made a decryption tool for one of their games.
Frontier going after my Rollercoaster Tycoon 3 modding tools (this one didnt actually happen but I heard some rumors that Frontier didnt like the tools I made and was considering legal action of some kind)

Anyone know of any more ideas?

Note that I am not talking about companies that use encryption or protection on their games or companies that have gone after someone for releasing things that are clearly cheats or purely for piracy, just companies that have gone after someone for reverse engineering their game for the purposes of making mods or for distributing mods for their game. (I am also not talking about mods that are shut down because of pure copyright violations like HaloGen)

Post by bgbennyboy » Sat May 23, 2009 9:59 pm

As Bugtest (and my old post) said, Telltale didn't issue me with a takedown or C+D. What actually happened was that I was dumb.

I had just finished making a new version of Telltale Explorer, which added support for reading and decrypting ttarchs/files (much like Bugtest's recent tool does). I'd spent ages on it and was pretty pleased with myself; I excitedly messaged a friend to share the good news, forgetting that he actually worked...at Telltale Games.

He was duty bound to tell his boss (the person who devised the ttarch and encryption), who asked me to send him an email before releasing it. He replied, explaining that he wasn't opposed to tools like it per-se but that the licence holders and investors required them to protect the resources. He requested that I not release it and was very amenable. His argument was essentially that an arms-race would ensue - with TT having to divert resources to further protecting the datafiles and away from making games.

As a big fan of Telltale's games and considering the reasonable way they dealt with the issue I agreed not to release it. Since then I've kept to a self-imposed rule of not including any Telltale resource decryption code in my tools.

What does the story show? Well essentially, that I can be an idiot, but also that not all companies are as bad as some of the others mentioned in this thread.

They didn't ask me to stop updating my tools though, I've continued to update my Telltale Music and Speech Extractor tools. I've just had to work around issues with the encryption.
